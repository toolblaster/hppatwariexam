Section 118: HP Tenancy & Land Reforms Act, 1972

A detailed analysis of the most crucial provision regulating land ownership in Himachal Pradesh, designed to protect the interests of local agriculturists while enabling controlled development.

1. Introduction & Objective

Section 118 of the Himachal Pradesh Tenancy and Land Reforms Act, 1972, is a protective legislation. Its primary objective is to preserve the limited agricultural land in the hilly terrain of Himachal Pradesh for the bonafide agriculturists of the state.

Key Purpose:

  • Prevent alienation of agricultural land to non-agriculturists.
  • Check land speculation and skyrocketing prices.
  • Ensure food security and preserve the agrarian economy.

2. The Core Provision

"No transfer of land including sales in execution of a decree of a civil court or for recovery of arrears of land revenues shall be valid in favour of a person who is not an agriculturist."

This essentially imposes a blanket ban on the transfer of land to non-agriculturists, with certain specific exceptions provided within the Act itself or through state government permission.

3. Who is an 'Agriculturist'?

Defined Under Section 2(2)

An "Agriculturist" means a landowner who:

  • Cultivates land personally.
  • Or cultivates through family members/servants.
  • Or receives rent (in cash or kind).

Important Nuance

Merely owning land does not automatically make one an agriculturist for the purpose of purchasing more land if that land is not within the state.

Crucial: One must be an agriculturist of Himachal Pradesh to buy agricultural land without permission.

4. Exemptions & Buying with Permission

While the general rule is prohibitive, Section 118(2) lists circumstances where non-agriculturists can acquire land, usually requiring permission from the State Government.

A. Residential Purpose

Non-agriculturists can purchase land for building a house. The maximum limit is currently 500 square meters. Permission is required.

B. Industrial/Commercial Use

Land can be purchased for setting up industries, tourism units (hotels), hydel projects, educational institutions, etc., subject to the essentiality certificate from the relevant department.

C. Inherited Land

Transfer by way of inheritance to legal heirs is generally allowed and does not attract the bar of Section 118.

5. Application Process (Rule 38A)

1

Application (Form LR-XIV)

Submitted to the Deputy Commissioner (DC) of the district where land is situated.

2

Verification

The DC sends the case to the Tehsildar/Revenue field staff to verify land details (Tatima, Jamabandi) and purpose.

3

Recommendation

The DC forwards the case with recommendations to the State Government (Revenue Department).

4

Cabinet/Government Approval

The State Government grants or refuses permission. If granted, a specific time frame (usually 2-3 years) is given to put the land to use.

6. Usage Restrictions & 'Vestment'

Permission under Section 118 is not absolute ownership in the traditional sense; it is conditional.

  • Change of Purpose: You cannot use land bought for industry to build a personal residence without fresh permission.
  • Time Limit: The purpose (e.g., building a factory or house) must be achieved within the stipulated period (e.g., 2 years).
  • Vesting in State: If the land is not used for the intended purpose within the time limit, or if the provision is violated, the land vests in the State Government free from all encumbrances.

7. Key Judicial Interpretations

Benami Transactions

Courts have taken a strict view on 'Benami' transactions where non-agriculturists buy land in the name of local agriculturists to bypass Section 118. Such transactions are void.

Will / Testament

Transfer via Will to a non-agriculturist stranger (not a legal heir) has often been contested. The consensus is that one cannot defeat the purpose of the Act through a Will to a stranger.
